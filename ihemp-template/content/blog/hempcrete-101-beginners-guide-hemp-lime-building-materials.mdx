If you've been paying attention to the sustainable building movement, you've probably started hearing the word "hempcrete" more often. It's showing up in architectural blogs, green building expos, and even mainstream home improvement conversations. But what exactly is it — and should you care?

This guide answers those questions, no construction background required.

## What Is Hempcrete?

Hempcrete is a biocomposite building material made by combining **hemp hurds** (the woody inner core of the hemp stalk, also called shivs), **lime binder**, and **water**. The result is a lightweight, breathable, and insulating material used primarily as wall infill in residential and commercial construction.

It is not structural — hempcrete does not carry load the way concrete or timber does. Instead, it fills frames and provides insulation, thermal mass, and moisture regulation. A timber or steel frame carries the structure; hempcrete wraps it.

The material has deep roots in European construction, with buildings in France and the UK using hemp-lime systems for more than three decades — among the earliest documented examples being the Maison de la Turque in Nogent-sur-Seine, France (c. 1986), which is widely cited as one of the first modern hemp-lime renovation projects. In the United States, interest has accelerated substantially since the passage of the [2018 Agriculture Improvement Act (Farm Bill)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/115th-congress/house-bill/2/text), which removed hemp — defined as *Cannabis sativa* L. with a delta-9 THC concentration of no more than 0.3% on a dry weight basis — from the federal Controlled Substances Act. That regulatory shift opened the door for domestic hemp farming and, downstream, domestic hempcrete production.

## How Is Hempcrete Used in Construction?

Hempcrete is most commonly applied in one of three forms:

### Cast-in-Place Hempcrete
The mix is poured or tamped into temporary formwork around a structural frame, then allowed to cure. This is common in new construction.

### Hempcrete Blocks and Panels
Pre-cast blocks or panels are manufactured off-site and assembled on location, similar to masonry units. This approach is growing in the U.S. because it simplifies the build process and reduces on-site labor.

### Hempcrete Spray Application
A wet mix is sprayed onto surfaces for wall infill or retrofitting existing structures. Less common in residential use, but applicable at scale.

## Why Builders and Homeowners Are Paying Attention

Hempcrete's appeal isn't one thing — it's a convergence of properties that conventional materials struggle to match simultaneously.

**Thermal performance:** Hemp-lime walls provide excellent thermal mass and insulation, reducing heating and cooling loads. The material absorbs heat slowly and releases it gradually, smoothing out temperature swings.

**Moisture management:** Hempcrete is breathable and hygroscopic — it absorbs and releases moisture without degrading. This passive humidity regulation reduces the risk of mold and improves indoor air quality naturally.

**Carbon sequestration:** Hemp absorbs CO₂ as it grows. When embedded in lime, a portion of that carbon is locked into the building for its lifetime. Some life-cycle analyses suggest hempcrete walls can achieve a net negative or very low carbon footprint compared to conventional insulation systems — for example, research published by the BRE Group (Building Research Establishment, UK) and data from the Hemp Industries Association have explored hempcrete's embodied carbon profile, though results vary by mix design, sourcing, and regional energy grid.

**Fire and pest resistance:** Lime-encased hemp hurds are not flammable under standard conditions, and the alkaline lime environment discourages pest activity.

## Hemp4Humanity and Real-World Hempcrete Projects

One of the most compelling demonstrations of hempcrete in practice comes from the work of **Cody Ley and Hemp4Humanity**, a nonprofit organization dedicated to using industrial hemp to build sustainable, affordable housing. Ley and his team have been at the forefront of applying hempcrete construction techniques in real communities — not just as demonstration builds, but as functional homes intended to address housing insecurity.

Hemp4Humanity projects have helped illustrate that hempcrete can be adapted for varying climates and builder skill levels, and that the material's supply chain — from hemp hurd sourcing to lime procurement — is increasingly viable in North America. Following projects like these is one of the best ways to track how hempcrete moves from niche material to mainstream adoption.

## Regulatory and Availability Context

As of publication (May 2026), domestic hemp production under the 2018 Farm Bill has expanded the supply of hemp biomass available for non-CBD industrial applications, including construction. However, availability of processed hemp hurds in the United States still varies by region. Readers should verify current sourcing options and any state-level building code requirements with local authorities having jurisdiction (AHJ) before beginning a hempcrete project — building codes are set at the state and local level and may not yet include specific hempcrete classifications in every jurisdiction.

The American Lime Technology and the [Hemp Industries Association](https://thehia.org/) are two organizations actively working on broader code adoption and industry standards. Regulations and product availability can change; always confirm current status before making procurement or design decisions.

## Getting Started

If you're exploring hempcrete for a project — or just educating yourself — here's where to begin:

- **Research your local building codes.** Contact your city or county building department and ask about non-standard wall assemblies.
- **Connect with practitioners.** Organizations like Hemp4Humanity and builders who have completed hempcrete projects are your most credible resources.
- **Source your materials thoughtfully.** Look for domestic hemp hurd suppliers and natural hydraulic lime (NHL) rated for your climate.
- **Consider a small test build.** Many people start with a small outbuilding, garden shed, or wall section before committing to a full home.

## Advocacy Starts With Education

Hemp's potential as a sustainable building material is one of its most powerful arguments for broader agricultural and industrial support. When the public understands that hemp can literally build homes — capturing carbon, resisting moisture, and lasting for generations — the conversation about hemp policy shifts from controversy to common sense.

To stay current on hemp legislation, hemp farming rights, and industrial hemp development in your state, explore the resources available throughout the iHemp network and connect with your state's hemp advocacy organizations.
