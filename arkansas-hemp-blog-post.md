The Arkansas hemp industry stands at a pivotal moment. Since the passage of Act 981 in 2017—the Arkansas Industrial Hemp Act—the state has witnessed rapid expansion, followed by market consolidation, and now a period of regulatory refinement. For hemp farmers, processors, and retailers across the Natural State, staying informed about the latest regulations, market trends, and compliance requirements is essential for sustainable operation.

This guide provides a comprehensive overview of the Arkansas hemp landscape in 2026, offering practical insights for both established operators and newcomers considering entry into this evolving agricultural sector.

## Arkansas’s Regulatory Framework: Act 981 and Beyond

### The Arkansas Industrial Hemp Act (Act 981)
The foundation of Arkansas’s hemp industry remains **Act 981 of 2017**, which established the legal framework for industrial hemp cultivation, processing, and handling. The Act defines industrial hemp as *Cannabis sativa L.* containing no more than 0.3% delta-9 tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) on a dry weight basis—aligning with the federal standard set by the 2018 Farm Bill.

The **Arkansas Department of Agriculture (ADA), Plant Industries Division** serves as the primary regulatory body overseeing hemp licensing and enforcement. The ADA issues two main license types:

1. **Grower License**: Authorizes cultivation of industrial hemp for commercial purposes.
2. **Processor/Handler License**: Permits receipt, storage, and processing of hemp materials for further refinement.

### Licensing Requirements and Compliance
To obtain a hemp license in Arkansas, applicants must meet several key requirements:

- **Site Verification**: Each growing location must be registered with GPS coordinates and approved by the ADA.
- **THC Testing**: All hemp crops must be tested by an ADA-approved laboratory to verify THC levels remain below 0.3%.
- **Background Checks**: License applicants undergo criminal history reviews.
- **Record-Keeping**: Detailed planting, harvesting, and sales records must be maintained for at least three years.
- **Annual Renewal**: Licenses expire on December 31 each year and require renewal.

**Important Note**: Only licensed growers and processors may legally possess hemp plants or materials in Arkansas. Unauthorized possession remains prohibited.

### Recent Regulatory Shift: Retail Oversight Transfers
In 2025, a significant administrative change occurred: **retail oversight of hemp-derived consumable products transitioned from the ADA to the Arkansas Tobacco Control Board (ATCB)**. This move reflects increased regulatory scrutiny of finished hemp products sold at retail, particularly those containing cannabinoids like CBD.

Businesses manufacturing, distributing, or selling hemp-derived consumables must now comply with ATCB regulations in addition to ADA requirements. This dual-layer oversight emphasizes the importance of thorough compliance checks for Arkansas hemp enterprises.

## Market Trends: Contraction and Strategic Pivot

### From Boom to Consolidation
Arkansas’s hemp market has undergone substantial transformation since its early days. In 2019, the state issued **125 grower licenses**—reflecting widespread enthusiasm following federal legalization. By 2025, however, that number had declined to approximately **15 active licensed growers**.

This contraction mirrors national trends where early market exuberance gave way to economic realities: oversupply, price volatility, and regulatory uncertainty. Many early entrants exited the market, while remaining operators have generally adopted more conservative, business-focused approaches.

### Shift from Floral to Fiber and Grain Production
A notable trend among Arkansas hemp growers is the **strategic pivot from floral hemp (grown for CBD extraction) toward fiber and grain production**. Several factors drive this shift:

- **Regulatory Certainty**: Fiber and grain hemp face fewer cannabinoid-related regulatory hurdles compared to floral hemp.
- **Market Stability**: Industrial applications for hemp fiber (textiles, construction materials, automotive composites) and grain (food products, animal feed) offer more predictable pricing and demand.
- **Lower Risk**: Fiber/grain varieties typically have lower THC concentrations, reducing compliance risks associated with THC testing.

This diversification aligns with Arkansas’s agricultural strengths and provides a more sustainable foundation for long-term industry growth.

### Geographic Concentration
Hemp cultivation in Arkansas remains **concentrated in the northwestern region** of the state, particularly in Washington, Benton, and Carroll counties. This concentration reflects favorable growing conditions, established agricultural infrastructure, and proximity to processing facilities.

## Legislative Updates: Key Changes Impacting Arkansas Hemp

### HB 533/SB 533: THC Limits for Consumable Products
The most significant legislative development affecting Arkansas’s hemp industry is **HB 533/SB 533**, enacted in April 2025. This legislation:

- **Caps THC Content**: Limits delta-9 THC in hemp-derived consumable products to **1 milligram per container** (regardless of container size).
- **Expands Regulatory Authority**: Grants the Arkansas Tobacco Control Board oversight over retail hemp products.
- **Implements Stricter Labeling Requirements**: Mandates comprehensive labeling including cannabinoid content, batch numbers, and manufacturer information.

**Practical Implications**: This law dramatically affects CBD products and other hemp-derived consumables sold in Arkansas. Businesses must reformulate products, update labeling, and ensure rigorous third-party testing to comply with the 1mg THC limit.

### Act 629 (2023): Regulatory Alignment
**Act 629 of 2023** updated Arkansas’s hemp regulations to better align with federal guidance from the USDA. Key provisions include:

- Clarified sampling and testing protocols
- Enhanced record-keeping requirements
- Streamlined licensing procedures for research institutions

While less headline-grabbing than HB 533, Act 629 provides important operational clarity for licensed hemp businesses.

### Federal Preemption Ruling: State Authority Upheld
In July 2025, the **8th Circuit Court of Appeals ruled that Arkansas’s hemp regulations are not preempted by federal law**. The court affirmed that states may impose stricter requirements than the 2018 Farm Bill, including outright bans on certain hemp products.

This ruling reinforces Arkansas’s authority to regulate hemp within its borders and suggests other states may follow similar paths. For multi-state operators, this underscores the necessity of state-by-state compliance verification.

### 2026 Farm Bill: Implications for Arkansas
Provisions of the **2026 Farm Bill** are scheduled to take effect in November 2026. While specific impacts on Arkansas remain uncertain, potential areas of change include:

- THC testing methodologies and thresholds
- Hemp insurance programs
- Research funding allocations
- Interstate commerce provisions

Arkansas hemp stakeholders should monitor Farm Bill developments closely and prepare for potential regulatory adjustments.

## Practical Guidance for Arkansas Hemp Businesses

### For Growers: Cultivation Best Practices
1. **License First, Plant Later**: Never begin cultivation before receiving your ADA grower license and site approval.
2. **Select Appropriate Genetics**: Choose hemp varieties suited to Arkansas’s climate and aligned with your target market (fiber, grain, or dual-purpose).
3. **Implement Robust Testing Protocols**: Schedule THC testing at appropriate growth stages to avoid compliance issues.
4. **Maintain Meticulous Records**: Document planting dates, inputs, harvest yields, and sales transactions.
5. **Explore Crop Insurance Options**: Investigate USDA Risk Management Agency programs covering hemp.

### For Processors and Handlers: Compliance Checklist
1. **Secure Proper Licensing**: Obtain both ADA processor/handler licenses and any required local permits.
2. **Implement Chain-of-Custody Documentation**: Track hemp material from source to finished product.
3. **Invest in Laboratory Testing**: Partner with ADA-approved labs for mandatory THC testing and consider additional cannabinoid profiling.
4. **Develop Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)**: Document all processing steps for quality control and regulatory compliance.
5. **Stay Informed on Retail Regulations**: Monitor ATCB requirements for any products destined for Arkansas retail shelves.

### For Retailers: Navigating the New Landscape
1. **Verify Product Compliance**: Ensure all hemp-derived consumables meet the 1mg THC per container limit mandated by HB 533.
2. **Request Certificates of Analysis (COAs)**: Obtain third-party lab reports from suppliers verifying cannabinoid content and purity.
3. **Train Staff on Legal Requirements**: Educate employees about hemp regulations and appropriate customer communications.
4. **Review Labeling Thoroughly**: Confirm all products include required information: ingredients, cannabinoid content, batch numbers, and manufacturer details.
5. **Maintain Sales Records**: Keep detailed records of hemp product purchases and sales for potential regulatory audits.

## The Path Forward: Opportunities in Arkansas Hemp

### Industrial Hemp: Building on Agricultural Strengths
Arkansas possesses significant advantages for industrial hemp production:

- **Favorable Growing Conditions**: Adequate rainfall, fertile soils, and suitable climate for fiber and grain hemp varieties.
- **Existing Infrastructure**: Established agricultural supply chains, processing facilities, and transportation networks.
- **Research Partnerships**: Collaboration opportunities with the University of Arkansas System Division of Agriculture and other institutions.

### Emerging Markets and Applications
Beyond traditional uses, Arkansas hemp businesses might explore:

- **Hemp-Based Construction Materials**: Hempcrete, insulation, and composite building products.
- **Sustainable Textiles**: Fiber processing for apparel and industrial fabrics.
- **Animal Bedding and Feed**: Utilizing hemp hurd and grain in livestock operations.
- **Biodegradable Plastics**: Hemp-based alternatives to petroleum-derived plastics.

### Advocacy and Industry Development
To support sustainable growth, Arkansas hemp stakeholders should consider:

- **Joining Industry Associations**: Organizations like the Arkansas Hemp Association provide networking, education, and advocacy.
- **Participating in Rulemaking**: Engaging with ADA and ATCB during public comment periods for proposed regulations.
- **Supporting Research Initiatives**: Collaborating with academic institutions on agronomic and economic studies.
- **Educating Consumers**: Providing accurate, compliant information about hemp products and their uses.
