## Executive Summary
Arkansas's hemp industry has undergone significant regulatory changes since its reintroduction in 2019. While initial enthusiasm led to over 120 licensed growers in 2020, the industry has contracted to approximately 15 active growers by 2025 due to market adjustments and new regulatory constraints. Recent legislation (HB 533/SB 533) has imposed strict THC limits on consumable hemp products, effectively reshaping the market landscape. Despite these challenges, Arkansas maintains a structured hemp program with clear licensing requirements and continues to explore industrial hemp applications for fiber and grain.

## 1. Official Regulations & Licensing

### Arkansas Department of Agriculture Hemp Program
- **Primary Regulatory Body:** Arkansas Department of Agriculture, Plant Industries Division, Quality Control and Compliance (Feed, Fertilizer, Hemp, Seed) Section
- **Program Authority:** Established under Act 981 (2017), "The Arkansas Industrial Hemp Act of 2017"
- **Licensing Structure:** Distinguishes between growers (cultivation) and processors/handlers (refining/storage)
- **Key Requirements:**
  - Site verification for all hemp plots
  - THC testing of all plots to ensure compliance with <0.3% THC threshold
  - License holders must possess all industrial hemp materials (viable seed, live plants, leaf, floral material)
- **Recent Oversight Shift:** As of 2025, retail and enforcement oversight transferred to Arkansas Tobacco Control Board (ATCB) for consumable hemp products
- **Program Stability:** Rules are reviewed annually, with most recent updates upheld through Act 629 (2023)

## 2. Recent Industry News (2024-2026)

### Market Contraction & Regulatory Enforcement
- **Declining Participation:** From 125 licensed growers in 2019 to approximately 15 in 2025
- **Enforcement Actions:** As of July 2025, regulatory agencies and local police actively enforce new hemp product restrictions
- **Industry Response:** Petitions and advocacy efforts to reverse restrictive legislation, with limited success

### Federal Court Ruling
- **July 2025:** Federal court ruled Arkansas's hemp ban is not preempted by federal law
- **Key Finding:** "Just because states may legalize hemp under the 2018 Farm Bill does not mean they must"
- **Impact:** Arkansas free to enforce its ban on intoxicating hemp-derived products

### 2026 Farm Bill Implications
- **Timeline:** 2025 Farm Bill provisions take full effect in November 2026
- **Potential Changes:** May further shape Arkansas hemp law, with ongoing debates about intoxicating hemp product bans
- **Industry Concerns:** Hemp Planting Predictability Act proposed to delay federal hemp ban by two years

## 5. Recent Legislative Updates

### HB 533 / SB 533 (2025)
- **Status:** Signed into law by Governor Sarah Huckabee Sanders
- **Key Provisions:**
  - Caps THC at 1mg per container for consumable hemp products
  - Transfers oversight to Tobacco Control Board
  - Effectively ends most consumable hemp market in Arkansas
- **Effective Date:** Enforcement active as of July 2025
- **Industry Impact:** Dramatically restricts access to hemp-derived THC products including gummies, vapes, tinctures, and topicals

### Act 629 (2023)
- **Purpose:** Updated hemp regulations in alignment with federal guidance
- **Status:** Upheld in 2025 as part of annual regulatory review

### Federal Preemption Ruling
- **July 2025:** 8th Circuit Court ruling allows Arkansas to enforce stricter hemp regulations than federal standards
- **Implication:** States have authority to impose additional restrictions beyond 2018 Farm Bill

## 6. Compliance Considerations for Content

### Regulatory Boundaries
- **THC Limits:** All hemp must contain <0.3% THC by dry weight
- **Product Restrictions:** Consumable hemp products limited to 1mg THC per container
- **Licensing Required:** All hemp material possession restricted to license holders
- **Federal Property:** Hemp-derived CBD prohibited on all federal properties within Arkansas

### Marketing Limitations
- **No Health Claims:** Strict prohibition on disease claims for CBD products
- **Proper Labeling:** Requires third-party Certificates of Analysis from ISO/IEC 17025 accredited labs
- **State-Federal Conflict:** Arkansas regulations may be stricter than federal standards
